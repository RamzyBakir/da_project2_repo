## The Effect of Music on Focus and Productivity While Studying

This project investigates the relationship between music consumption habits and self-reported focus and productivity levels during study sessions. Using survey data, it explores how different genres, characteristics (lyrical vs. non-lyrical), and environmental factors influence deep-focus tasks.

### Project Structure

- `Project_II_Music_While_Studying_vs_Focus.ipynb`: The main Jupyter notebook containing data cleaning, exploratory data analysis (EDA), and statistical testing.
- `Cleaned_Music_Study_Data.csv`: The processed dataset used for analysis.
- `The Effect of Music on Focus and Productivity While Studying Report.docx`: A detailed formal report of the findings.

### Main Goals

- Investigate the relationship between music habits and productivity.
- Identify genres and music characteristics (e.g., lyrical vs. non-lyrical) conducive to deep focus.
- Determine if music acts as a shield against environmental distractions or as a source of distraction itself.

### Data Analysis Steps

1.  **Data Cleaning & Preparation:** Handling missing values, renaming survey questions for programmatic ease, and standardizing categorical data.
2.  **Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA):** Visualizing demographics (age, gender), study habits, and performance metrics (productivity vs. distraction scores).
3.  **Statistical Testing:** Conducting T-tests to compare distraction levels between lyrical and non-lyrical music and generating correlation matrices.

### Key Findings

- **Genre Trends:** Pop, Rap, and Instrumental music are the most popular choices for studying.
- **Productivity & Focus:** There is a positive correlation between music's "focus shielding" capability and overall productivity.
- **Distraction Levels:** No statistically significant difference in distraction levels was found between lyrical and non-lyrical music in this sample (p-value: 0.466).
- **Task Specificity:** Music preference varies by task type, with a higher preference for music during repetitive writing tasks compared to high-cognition memorization.
